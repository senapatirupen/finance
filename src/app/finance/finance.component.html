Inflation: The purchasing power of a currency reduces over time due to the effect of inflation. Inflation is the increase in the general price level of goods and services in an economy, which means that the same amount of currency buys fewer goods and services than it did in the past.

Let's break down some example to understand how inflation and interest rates impact various aspects of the economy:

Fixed Deposit Interest Rate (6%): Suppose a bank offers a fixed deposit with an annual interest rate of 6%. If the inflation rate in the economy is also around 6%, it means that the real return on the fixed deposit is close to zero. In other words, the purchasing power of the money deposited in the fixed deposit remains relatively stable, but it does not provide significant real growth.

Example: If you deposit ₹100,000 in a fixed deposit with a 6% interest rate for a year, you will earn ₹6,000 in interest. However, due to the 6% inflation rate, the value of ₹106,000 after one year may not have increased significantly in terms of purchasing power.

Home Loan Interest Rate (7%): When a bank offers a home loan at a 7% interest rate, it means that borrowers will have to pay an additional 7% on top of the principal amount borrowed. The higher the interest rate, the higher the overall cost of the home loan.

Example: If you take a home loan of ₹1,000,000 at a 7% interest rate for 20 years, you will have to pay ₹1,000,000 (principal) + ₹700,000 (interest) = ₹1,700,000 in total over the loan tenure. However, if the home price inflation rate is also around 7%, the value of the property you purchase may have increased by a similar percentage over the same period.

Education Loan Interest Rate (10%): When a bank provides an education loan at a 10% interest rate, students or their parents will need to repay the loan with an additional 10% interest on top of the borrowed amount.

Example: If you take an education loan of ₹500,000 at a 10% interest rate for 5 years, you will have to repay ₹500,000 (principal) + ₹250,000 (interest) = ₹750,000. However, if the education expenditure inflation rate is also around 10%, the overall cost of education may have increased by a similar percentage over the same period.

Gold Loan Interest Rate (8%): A gold loan is secured by gold, and the interest rate is typically lower compared to other types of loans.

Example: If you take a gold loan of ₹200,000 at an 8% interest rate for 1 year, you will have to repay ₹200,000 (principal) + ₹16,000 (interest) = ₹216,000. However, if the gold buying price inflation rate is also around 8%, the value of the gold you pledge as collateral may have increased by a similar percentage over the same period.

Car Loan Interest Rate (9%): When a bank provides a car loan at a 9% interest rate, borrowers will need to repay the loan with an additional 9% interest on top of the principal amount borrowed.

Example: If you take a car loan of ₹600,000 at a 9% interest rate for 3 years, you will have to repay ₹600,000 (principal) + ₹162,000 (interest) = ₹762,000. However, if the car buying price inflation rate is also around 9%, the cost of the car you want to buy may have increased by a similar percentage over the same period.

In each of these examples, the impact of inflation on interest rates is evident. The rates at which loans are provided and the returns on deposits are influenced by the prevailing inflation rates in the economy. It is essential to consider inflation while making financial decisions to understand the real value of money and assets over time.

Buy Low Strategy: When the price of the stock or the Net Asset Value (NAV) of a mutual fund you've invested in drops by a predetermined percentage (e.g., 2%), you plan to invest more funds into that asset. This is based on the idea that the asset is now "on sale" and might present a better buying opportunity.

Sell High or Enjoy Gains: Once the value of the asset increases, you may choose to sell some of your holdings to take advantage of the price appreciation. Alternatively, you can hold onto the asset and enjoy the gains, allowing your investment to potentially grow further.

Regular Monthly Investments: Additionally, you're planning to invest a fixed amount of money on a regular monthly basis. This practice is known as dollar-cost averaging, where you invest a consistent amount regardless of market conditions. This can help smooth out the impact of market volatility over time.

SGBs are government securities denominated in grams of gold. They are substitutes for holding physical gold. Investors have to pay the issue price in cash and the bonds will be redeemed in cash on maturity. The Bond is issued by Reserve Bank on behalf of Government of India.

The quantity of gold for which the investor pays is protected, since he receives the ongoing market price at the time of redemption/ premature redemption. The SGB offers a superior alternative to holding gold in physical form. The risks and costs of storage are eliminated. Investors are assured of the market value of gold at the time of maturity and periodical interest. SGB is free from issues like making charges and purity in the case of gold in jewellery form. The bonds are held in the books of the RBI or in demat form eliminating risk of loss of scrip etc.

The Bonds bear interest at the rate of 2.50 per cent (fixed rate) per annum on the amount of initial investment. Interest will be credited semi-annually to the bank account of the investor and the last interest will be payable on maturity along with the principal.

Credit Card Payment

Don’t leave any amount outstanding on your credit card.

Let's say you have a credit bill of Rs 20,100 due on 1 May. And you paid Rs 20,000. You're thinking 'Oh, 3 per cent a month but only on Rs 100, no? I can pay.' You're in for a big surprise.

Your bill date was probably 20 April. After that, you would have had some Swiggy bills, some other purchases, etc. on the cars. That's, say, another Rs 11,000.

You'll get charged 3 per cent on the Rs 100 you didn't pay the last time, sure.

You'll also get charged 3 per cent for the Rs 11,000!

Once you have an outstanding amount left after the due date, even one rupee, you pay interest from the day you transacted. Even if that transaction was BEFORE the due date.

Also, any MORE transactions attract interest from day one.

If you were unaware and bought some Rs 9000 worth of items on 10 May. Now you have Rs 20,100 outstanding. Each purchase is charged interest from the day you purchased it.

So on your next bill on 20 May, you'll see an interest charge of about Rs 600.

They also add GST, so you pay nearly Rs 800 extra. Your bill is now Rs 21,000.

Just because you 'revolved' Rs 100.

Sweep-in Fixed Deposits (FDs)

Sweep-in Fixed Deposits (FDs) are a financial product that allows you to link a savings or current account with a fixed deposit. The concept is to maintain a higher balance in the savings or current account while earning higher interest on the surplus amount through an FD. Here's how it typically works, illustrated with an example:

Let's say you have a savings account with a linked sweep-in FD facility. The minimum threshold for auto-sweep is set at Rs 25,000. Any amount above this threshold in your savings account gets automatically transferred to an FD in multiples of Rs 1,000.

The system automatically converts the surplus amount above to (Rs 25,000) into a fixed deposit. You now have an FD of surplus amount created with an interest rate of 6% per annum.

The sweep-in FD allows easy access to funds when needed, as it can be automatically broken to meet the requirements of the savings account.

It's important to note that the specific terms and conditions of sweep-in FDs may vary among banks, and individuals should carefully review the features and charges associated with the product.
