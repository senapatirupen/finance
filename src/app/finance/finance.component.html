Why To Invest In Stock Market

Inflation: The purchasing power of a currency reduces over time due to the effect of inflation. Inflation is the increase in the general price level of goods and services in an economy, which means that the same amount of currency buys fewer goods and services than it did in the past.

Let's break down some example to understand how inflation and interest rates impact various aspects of the economy:

Fixed Deposit Interest Rate (6%): Suppose a bank offers a fixed deposit with an annual interest rate of 6%. If the inflation rate in the economy is also around 6%, it means that the real return on the fixed deposit is close to zero. In other words, the purchasing power of the money deposited in the fixed deposit remains relatively stable, but it does not provide significant real growth.

Example: If you deposit ₹100,000 in a fixed deposit with a 6% interest rate for a year, you will earn ₹6,000 in interest. However, due to the 6% inflation rate, the value of ₹106,000 after one year may not have increased significantly in terms of purchasing power.

Home Loan Interest Rate (7%): When a bank offers a home loan at a 7% interest rate, it means that borrowers will have to pay an additional 7% on top of the principal amount borrowed. The higher the interest rate, the higher the overall cost of the home loan.

Example: If you take a home loan of ₹1,000,000 at a 7% interest rate for 20 years, you will have to pay ₹1,000,000 (principal) + ₹700,000 (interest) = ₹1,700,000 in total over the loan tenure. However, if the home price inflation rate is also around 7%, the value of the property you purchase may have increased by a similar percentage over the same period.

Education Loan Interest Rate (10%): When a bank provides an education loan at a 10% interest rate, students or their parents will need to repay the loan with an additional 10% interest on top of the borrowed amount.

Example: If you take an education loan of ₹500,000 at a 10% interest rate for 5 years, you will have to repay ₹500,000 (principal) + ₹250,000 (interest) = ₹750,000. However, if the education expenditure inflation rate is also around 10%, the overall cost of education may have increased by a similar percentage over the same period.

Gold Loan Interest Rate (8%): A gold loan is secured by gold, and the interest rate is typically lower compared to other types of loans.

Example: If you take a gold loan of ₹200,000 at an 8% interest rate for 1 year, you will have to repay ₹200,000 (principal) + ₹16,000 (interest) = ₹216,000. However, if the gold buying price inflation rate is also around 8%, the value of the gold you pledge as collateral may have increased by a similar percentage over the same period.

Car Loan Interest Rate (9%): When a bank provides a car loan at a 9% interest rate, borrowers will need to repay the loan with an additional 9% interest on top of the principal amount borrowed.

Example: If you take a car loan of ₹600,000 at a 9% interest rate for 3 years, you will have to repay ₹600,000 (principal) + ₹162,000 (interest) = ₹762,000. However, if the car buying price inflation rate is also around 9%, the cost of the car you want to buy may have increased by a similar percentage over the same period.

In each of these examples, the impact of inflation on interest rates is evident. The rates at which loans are provided and the returns on deposits are influenced by the prevailing inflation rates in the economy. It is essential to consider inflation while making financial decisions to understand the real value of money and assets over time.
